Marine Life at Vleesbaai

Vleesbaai's coastal waters and shores are teeming with diverse marine life, making it an exciting destination for nature lovers and families alike. Timing your visit just right, especially during the months of September and October, offers a chance to witness Southern Right whales with their calves taking shelter in the bay. The whales come in close to shore, providing a once-in-a-lifetime spectacle as they breach and play in the protected waters.

Throughout the year, schools of dolphins are also frequently seen in the bay, gliding through the water in playful groups. On land, the rare and distinctive Oystercatcher bird is another fascinating sight. With their vibrant red beaks and shy demeanor, these birds are often seen along quiet beaches, feeding on mussels rather than oysters as their name suggests. Oystercatchers are known for their monogamous nature, living up to 40 years and staying loyal to a single partner.

In the intertidal zone, which lies between the low and high watermarks, a rich variety of marine species awaits discovery. This area is a natural wonderland for young explorers, where tide pools reveal a vibrant array of creatures including anemones, barnacles, sea stars, mussels, snails, and small fish. Every year, the rangers of Fransmanshoek Conservancy host an educational Intertidal Day during the holiday season, offering visitors a chance to learn more about the marine life inhabiting the rocky shores of Vleesbaai.

For those who enjoy beachcombing, the shores of Vleesbaai towards Springerbaai are perfect for searching for Pansy shells and the elusive Nautilus. Meanwhile, wild oysters can still be found clinging to the rocks during low tide, though their numbers have dwindled due to overharvesting.

Visitors should also be mindful of the local wildlife when traveling around the area. Tortoises and small buck are often spotted crossing the roads, reminding us of the delicate balance between humans and nature in this beautiful coastal region.
